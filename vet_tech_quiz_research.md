## CATEGORY 1: DENTAL PROCEDURES

### Q1.1
**During a veterinary dental prophylaxis, what is the correct order of the major procedural steps after the patient is anesthetized and intubated?**
- ✓ Oral exam → gross calculus removal → supragingival scaling → subgingival scaling → polishing → probing/charting → irrigation → radiographs
- ✗ Polishing → scaling → probing → radiographs → irrigation
- ✗ Radiographs → scaling → polishing → probing → irrigation
- ✗ Supragingival scaling → polishing → subgingival scaling → oral exam → radiographs

**Explanation:** The standard sequence starts with an oral exam to note obvious pathology, then removes gross calculus before detailed scaling (supra- then subgingival), polishing to smooth micro-scratches, probing/charting to document pocket depths, irrigation to flush debris, and finally radiographs to evaluate structures below the gumline.

---

### Q1.2
**Why is polishing performed immediately after scaling during a dental prophylaxis?**
- ✓ To smooth micro-scratches in the enamel left by the scaler, which would otherwise promote rapid plaque re-accumulation
- ✗ To apply fluoride treatment to the teeth
- ✗ To remove subgingival calculus that the scaler missed
- ✗ To sterilize the tooth surface and kill remaining bacteria

**Explanation:** Scaling creates microscopic roughness on the enamel surface. Polishing smooths these scratches so plaque cannot adhere as easily, slowing future calculus formation.

---

### Q1.3
**In the Modified Triadan numbering system, what does tooth number 409 represent?**
- ✓ Lower right first molar
- ✗ Upper right canine
- ✗ Lower left fourth premolar
- ✗ Upper left first molar

**Explanation:** In the Modified Triadan system, the first digit represents the quadrant (1=upper right, 2=upper left, 3=lower left, 4=lower right). The second and third digits represent tooth position from the midline. So 409 = quadrant 4 (lower right), tooth position 09 (first molar).

---

### Q1.4
**A furcation grade of F2 on a dental chart indicates:**
- ✓ The periodontal probe enters the furcation more than one-third of the tooth width, but does not pass through entirely
- ✗ No furcation involvement is present
- ✗ The probe just barely enters the furcation (less than one-third of tooth width)
- ✗ The probe passes completely through the furcation from one side to the other

**Explanation:** Furcation grading: F0 = no involvement, F1 = probe enters <1/3 width, F2 = probe enters >1/3 width but not through-and-through, F3 = probe passes entirely through. F2 indicates significant but not complete bone loss between the roots.

---

### Q1.5
**What does a tooth mobility grade of M3 indicate?**
- ✓ Severe mobility with >1mm horizontal movement AND/OR vertical (depressible) movement
- ✗ No detectable movement
- ✗ Slight mobility with less than 1mm horizontal movement
- ✗ Moderate mobility with >1mm horizontal movement but no vertical movement

**Explanation:** M0 = no movement, M1 = slight (<1mm horizontal), M2 = moderate (>1mm horizontal only), M3 = severe (>1mm horizontal AND/OR vertical movement into the alveolus). M3 teeth often require extraction.

---

### Q1.6
**An adult dog has how many permanent teeth?**
- ✓ 42
- ✗ 30
- ✗ 38
- ✗ 44

**Explanation:** Adult dogs have 42 permanent teeth: 12 incisors, 4 canines, 16 premolars, and 10 molars. Adult cats, by contrast, have 30 permanent teeth.

---

### Q1.7
**An adult cat has how many permanent teeth?**
- ✓ 30
- ✗ 42
- ✗ 28
- ✗ 36

**Explanation:** Adult cats have 30 permanent teeth. They are missing upper and lower first premolars and the lower second premolar present in dogs, and have fewer molars.

---

### Q1.8
**During ultrasonic scaling, what must be continuously applied to the scaler tip to prevent thermal damage to the tooth?**
- ✓ A constant water spray (lavage)
- ✗ Polishing paste
- ✗ Chlorhexidine solution only
- ✗ Compressed air

**Explanation:** Ultrasonic scalers generate significant heat through vibration. Continuous water lavage is essential to cool the tip and the tooth surface, preventing thermal necrosis of the pulp.

---

### Q1.9
**Why is a cuffed endotracheal tube particularly important during dental procedures?**
- ✓ To prevent aspiration of water, blood, debris, and bacteria into the lungs during scaling and irrigation
- ✗ To deliver dental anesthetic gas directly to the oral cavity
- ✗ To keep the mouth propped open for the procedure
- ✗ To monitor end-tidal CO2 during the procedure (though this is a secondary benefit)

**Explanation:** Dental procedures generate large amounts of aerosolized water, calculus debris, blood, and bacteria. A properly inflated cuffed ET tube seals the trachea and prevents aspiration pneumonia.

---

### Q1.10
**What instrument is used to measure gingival sulcus depth during periodontal probing?**
- ✓ A graduated periodontal probe (e.g., Williams probe)
- ✗ A dental explorer
- ✗ A curette
- ✗ A dental elevator

**Explanation:** A periodontal probe has millimeter markings along its tip and is gently inserted into the gingival sulcus to measure pocket depth. A normal sulcus is 1-3mm in dogs and 0.5-1mm in cats.

---

### Q1.11
**Normal gingival sulcus depth in a healthy dog is:**
- ✓ 1–3 mm
- ✗ 0–0.5 mm
- ✗ 4–6 mm
- ✗ 7–9 mm

**Explanation:** Normal canine sulcus depth is 1-3mm. In cats, it is 0.5-1mm. Depths exceeding these ranges indicate periodontal pocketing and disease requiring further evaluation.

---

### Q1.12
**Which dental instrument is used to loosen the periodontal ligament before tooth extraction?**
- ✓ Dental elevator (luxator)
- ✗ Periodontal probe
- ✗ Ultrasonic scaler
- ✗ Polishing cup

**Explanation:** Dental elevators and luxators are inserted between the tooth root and alveolar bone to stretch and tear the periodontal ligament, loosening the tooth before extraction with forceps.

## CATEGORY 2: VACCINE ADMINISTRATION

### Q2.1
**When administering a rabies vaccine to a cat, which limb should be used and where?**
- ✓ Right rear limb, as distal as possible (below the stifle)
- ✗ Left rear limb, as distal as possible
- ✗ Between the shoulder blades (interscapular region)
- ✗ Right front limb, below the elbow

**Explanation:** Per AAFP guidelines, the rabies vaccine is given in the right rear limb as distally as possible. This allows tracking of vaccine-associated sarcomas (VAS) to specific vaccines, and distal limb placement allows for amputation with better prognosis if a sarcoma develops.

---

### Q2.2
**Where should the Feline Leukemia Virus (FeLV) vaccine be administered in cats?**
- ✓ Left rear limb, as distal as possible (below the stifle)
- ✗ Right rear limb, below the stifle
- ✗ Between the shoulder blades
- ✗ Left front limb, below the elbow

**Explanation:** AAFP guidelines assign the left rear limb for FeLV vaccine. The mnemonic: Right Rear = Rabies, Left Rear = Leukemia (FeLV), Right Front = FVRCP. Each vaccine gets a distinct location to trace any sarcoma to the causative vaccine.

---

### Q2.3
**Where should the FVRCP (core feline) vaccine be administered?**
- ✓ Right front limb, as distal as possible (below the elbow)
- ✗ Left front limb, below the elbow
- ✗ Right rear limb, below the stifle
- ✗ Interscapular region

**Explanation:** The FVRCP vaccine is administered in the right front limb per AAFP guidelines. This keeps each vaccine in a distinct anatomical location for sarcoma tracking purposes.

---

### Q2.4
**Why should vaccines in cats NO LONGER be administered in the interscapular (between the shoulder blades) region?**
- ✓ If a vaccine-associated sarcoma develops there, surgical removal with adequate margins is extremely difficult due to the anatomy
- ✗ The interscapular region has poor blood supply, reducing vaccine efficacy
- ✗ Cats are more likely to have an allergic reaction at this location
- ✗ The vaccine cannot be absorbed properly through the thick skin in that area

**Explanation:** Vaccine-associated sarcomas (VAS) are aggressive tumors that require wide surgical margins (3-5cm in all directions). The interscapular area overlies the spine and scapulae, making complete excision nearly impossible. Distal limb locations allow amputation as a curative option.

---

### Q2.5
**Which of the following is a core vaccine for dogs?**
- ✓ DAPP (Distemper, Adenovirus, Parvovirus, Parainfluenza)
- ✗ Bordetella bronchiseptica
- ✗ Leptospirosis
- ✗ Canine Influenza (H3N2/H3N8)

**Explanation:** Core vaccines are recommended for ALL dogs regardless of lifestyle. DAPP and Rabies are core canine vaccines. Bordetella, Leptospirosis, Lyme, and Canine Influenza are non-core (lifestyle-dependent).

---

### Q2.6
**At what age should a puppy typically begin the DAPP vaccination series?**
- ✓ 6–8 weeks of age
- ✗ 2 weeks of age
- ✗ 12–16 weeks of age (single dose only)
- ✗ 6 months of age

**Explanation:** The DAPP series begins at 6-8 weeks, with boosters every 3-4 weeks until at least 16 weeks of age. This timing accounts for the decline of maternal antibodies while building the puppy's own immune response.

---

### Q2.7
**How often should DAPP boosters be given during the puppy vaccination series?**
- ✓ Every 3–4 weeks until at least 16 weeks of age
- ✗ Once at 8 weeks and once at 16 weeks only
- ✗ Weekly from 6 to 16 weeks
- ✗ Every 6 months for the first year

**Explanation:** Puppies receive DAPP every 3-4 weeks (typically at 6-8, 10-12, and 14-16 weeks). Multiple boosters are needed because maternal antibodies can interfere with vaccine response, and their decline varies between individuals.

---

### Q2.8
**The Bordetella bronchiseptica vaccine for dogs can be given by which route(s)?**
- ✓ Intranasal, oral, or injectable (subcutaneous) — depending on the product
- ✗ Intravenous only
- ✗ Intramuscular only
- ✗ Topical (applied to the skin)

**Explanation:** Bordetella vaccines come in intranasal, oral, and injectable formulations. The intranasal and oral routes provide rapid local mucosal immunity and are often preferred for dogs entering boarding or daycare situations.

---

### Q2.9
**Which of the following is a core vaccine for cats?**
- ✓ FVRCP (Feline Viral Rhinotracheitis, Calicivirus, Panleukopenia)
- ✗ FeLV (Feline Leukemia Virus)
- ✗ FIV (Feline Immunodeficiency Virus)
- ✗ Chlamydophila felis

**Explanation:** Core cat vaccines include FVRCP and Rabies. FeLV is considered core for kittens but non-core for adult indoor-only cats. FIV and Chlamydophila are non-core.

---

### Q2.10
**A cat develops facial swelling, vomiting, and hives within 30 minutes of vaccination. This is most likely:**
- ✓ A type I hypersensitivity (anaphylactic) reaction requiring immediate treatment with epinephrine
- ✗ A normal immune response that will resolve on its own
- ✗ A vaccine-associated sarcoma
- ✗ A delayed type IV hypersensitivity reaction

**Explanation:** Acute signs (facial swelling, urticaria, vomiting) within minutes to hours of vaccination indicate a type I (immediate) hypersensitivity reaction. Treatment includes epinephrine, diphenhydramine, and supportive care. Sarcomas develop weeks to months later.

---

### Q2.11
**What is the earliest age at which a puppy or kitten can typically receive a rabies vaccine?**
- ✓ 12–16 weeks of age (varies by local law and product label)
- ✗ 4 weeks of age
- ✗ 6 months of age
- ✗ 1 year of age

**Explanation:** Most jurisdictions and vaccine labels specify the first rabies vaccine at 12-16 weeks of age. A booster is given 1 year later, then every 1-3 years depending on the product and local regulations.

---

### Q2.12
**Most vaccines in dogs are administered by which route?**
- ✓ Subcutaneous (SQ) injection
- ✗ Intravenous (IV) injection
- ✗ Intramuscular (IM) injection in all cases
- ✗ Intradermal injection

**Explanation:** The majority of canine and feline vaccines are given subcutaneously. Some specific products (like certain rabies vaccines) may be labeled for IM use. Vaccines are never given IV.

## CATEGORY 3: ANESTHESIA & MONITORING

### Q3.1
**What is the normal SpO2 range for a dog or cat under anesthesia?**
- ✓ 95–100%
- ✗ 85–90%
- ✗ 75–80%
- ✗ 90–93%

**Explanation:** SpO2 (peripheral oxygen saturation) should be 95-100% under anesthesia. Values below 94% are concerning, and below 90% indicates significant hypoxemia requiring immediate intervention (check ET tube, increase O2 flow, manual ventilation).

---

### Q3.2
**What is the normal end-tidal CO2 (ETCO2) range during anesthesia in dogs and cats?**
- ✓ 35–45 mmHg
- ✗ 10–20 mmHg
- ✗ 50–65 mmHg
- ✗ 70–80 mmHg

**Explanation:** Normal ETCO2 is 35-45 mmHg. Values below 30 mmHg suggest hyperventilation, while values above 55 mmHg indicate hypoventilation (consider assisting ventilation or checking for airway obstruction).

---

### Q3.3
**What is the normal heart rate range for a cat under anesthesia?**
- ✓ 120–180 bpm
- ✗ 60–80 bpm
- ✗ 200–260 bpm
- ✗ 40–60 bpm

**Explanation:** Anesthetized cats normally maintain a heart rate of 120-180 bpm. Rates below 100 bpm in a cat are concerning for significant bradycardia and may require atropine or glycopyrrolate.

---

### Q3.4
**During anesthesia, the eye of a dog or cat rotates ventromedially (downward and inward). This indicates the patient is at which depth?**
- ✓ Stage III, Plane 2 — appropriate surgical depth
- ✗ Stage I — analgesia only, too light for surgery
- ✗ Stage II — excitement phase
- ✗ Stage III, Plane 4 — dangerously deep, near overdose

**Explanation:** Ventromedial eye rotation is a hallmark of Stage III, Plane 2, which is the ideal surgical plane. At this depth, the palpebral reflex is absent but the corneal reflex remains. If the eye returns to central position, the patient may be either too light or too deep.

---

### Q3.5
**Which reflex, if LOST, indicates that the patient is dangerously deep under anesthesia?**
- ✓ Corneal reflex
- ✗ Palpebral (eyelid) reflex
- ✗ Pedal withdrawal reflex
- ✗ Swallowing reflex

**Explanation:** The corneal reflex is one of the last reflexes lost and its absence indicates Stage III, Plane 4 or Stage IV (overdose), which is life-threatening. The palpebral reflex is normally absent at surgical depth (Plane 2) and is not cause for alarm.

---

### Q3.6
**Atipamezole is the reversal agent for which anesthetic drug?**
- ✓ Dexmedetomidine (alpha-2 adrenergic agonist)
- ✗ Midazolam (benzodiazepine)
- ✗ Hydromorphone (opioid)
- ✗ Propofol (IV induction agent)

**Explanation:** Atipamezole is a highly selective alpha-2 antagonist that reverses the sedative and cardiovascular effects of alpha-2 agonists like dexmedetomidine and medetomidine. It provides rapid, reliable reversal.

---

### Q3.7
**Naloxone is the reversal agent for which class of drugs?**
- ✓ Opioids (morphine, fentanyl, hydromorphone, etc.)
- ✗ Alpha-2 agonists (dexmedetomidine)
- ✗ Benzodiazepines (diazepam, midazolam)
- ✗ Phenothiazines (acepromazine)

**Explanation:** Naloxone is a competitive opioid antagonist that reverses respiratory depression, sedation, and analgesia from opioids. Important caveat: it also reverses pain control, so patients may need alternative analgesia.

---

### Q3.8
**Flumazenil is the reversal agent for which class of drugs?**
- ✓ Benzodiazepines (diazepam, midazolam)
- ✗ Opioids (morphine, fentanyl)
- ✗ Alpha-2 agonists (dexmedetomidine)
- ✗ Barbiturates (pentobarbital)

**Explanation:** Flumazenil competitively binds to central benzodiazepine receptors, reversing sedation, muscle relaxation, and anxiolysis produced by diazepam and midazolam.

---

### Q3.9
**If a patient under anesthesia becomes bradycardic (dangerously low heart rate), which drug is most commonly administered as a first-line treatment?**
- ✓ Atropine (anticholinergic)
- ✗ Epinephrine
- ✗ Propofol
- ✗ Dexmedetomidine

**Explanation:** Atropine is an anticholinergic that blocks vagal tone, increasing heart rate. Glycopyrrolate is another option. Epinephrine is reserved for cardiac arrest. Dexmedetomidine would worsen bradycardia as it is an alpha-2 agonist.

---

### Q3.10
**Stage II of anesthesia is also known as the:**
- ✓ Excitement stage — characterized by involuntary movement, irregular breathing, and increased reflexes
- ✗ Surgical anesthesia stage — ideal for procedures
- ✗ Analgesia stage — conscious but with reduced pain perception
- ✗ Overdose stage — respiratory and cardiovascular depression

**Explanation:** Stage II is the excitement phase, which should be passed through as quickly as possible. It is characterized by erratic breathing, involuntary limb movement, paddling, vocalization, and heightened reflexes. Patients should not be stimulated during this stage.

---

### Q3.11
**What should you do FIRST if you notice a patient under anesthesia has a SpO2 of 88%?**
- ✓ Check the ET tube for kinking or obstruction, verify O2 flow, and begin manual ventilation (bagging)
- ✗ Immediately administer epinephrine
- ✗ Increase the vaporizer setting to deepen anesthesia
- ✗ Turn off the anesthetic and wait for the patient to wake up

**Explanation:** SpO2 of 88% is significant hypoxemia. The first step is to ensure adequate oxygen delivery: check the ET tube patency, confirm the oxygen source is flowing, and assist ventilation by squeezing the reservoir bag. Increasing anesthetic depth would worsen the situation.

---

### Q3.12
**Which of the following is NOT typically used as a pre-anesthetic medication?**
- ✓ Propofol — it is an induction agent, not a pre-medication
- ✗ Dexmedetomidine (alpha-2 agonist sedative)
- ✗ Hydromorphone (opioid analgesic)
- ✗ Acepromazine (phenothiazine tranquilizer)

**Explanation:** Propofol is a rapid-acting IV induction agent used to induce general anesthesia, not as a premedicant. Common premedicants include sedatives (dexmedetomidine, acepromazine), opioids (hydromorphone, butorphanol), and anticholinergics (atropine, glycopyrrolate).

## CATEGORY 4: PHARMACOLOGY & DRUG CALCULATIONS

### Q4.1
**A veterinarian orders Drug X at 5 mg/kg for a 20 kg dog. The drug concentration is 50 mg/mL. How many mL should you draw up?**
- ✓ 2 mL
- ✗ 5 mL
- ✗ 1 mL
- ✗ 10 mL

**Explanation:** Dose = 20 kg × 5 mg/kg = 100 mg. Volume = 100 mg ÷ 50 mg/mL = 2 mL. The formula is: Volume (mL) = [Weight (kg) × Dose (mg/kg)] ÷ Concentration (mg/mL).

---

### Q4.2
**What is the standard maintenance fluid rate for a dog?**
- ✓ Approximately 2–2.5 mL/kg/hr (or 50–60 mL/kg/day)
- ✗ 10 mL/kg/hr
- ✗ 0.5 mL/kg/hr
- ✗ 100 mL/kg/day

**Explanation:** Maintenance fluid rate for dogs is approximately 50-60 mL/kg/day, which calculates to roughly 2-2.5 mL/kg/hr. For a 20 kg dog, this is about 40-50 mL/hr. Cats require slightly less at 40-50 mL/kg/day.

---

### Q4.3
**What is the IV crystalloid shock dose (total volume) for a dog?**
- ✓ Up to 90 mL/kg, given in incremental boluses of 10–20 mL/kg
- ✗ 5 mL/kg total
- ✗ Up to 200 mL/kg
- ✗ 1 mL/kg/hr

**Explanation:** The crystalloid shock dose for dogs is up to 90 mL/kg. It is given in 10-20 mL/kg boluses over 15-20 minutes, reassessing perfusion parameters between each bolus. For cats, the total shock dose is lower at approximately 60 mL/kg.

---

### Q4.4
**What is the IV crystalloid shock dose for a cat?**
- ✓ Up to 60 mL/kg, given in incremental boluses of 5–10 mL/kg
- ✗ Up to 90 mL/kg (same as dogs)
- ✗ Up to 200 mL/kg
- ✗ 1 mL/kg single bolus only

**Explanation:** Cats are more susceptible to fluid overload than dogs. Their crystalloid shock dose is 60 mL/kg maximum, given in smaller 5-10 mL/kg boluses with careful reassessment. Volume overload can lead to pulmonary edema.

---

### Q4.5
**Ketamine is classified under which DEA controlled substance schedule?**
- ✓ Schedule III
- ✗ Schedule II
- ✗ Schedule IV
- ✗ Schedule V

**Explanation:** Ketamine is a Schedule III controlled substance. It has moderate to low potential for physical and psychological dependence. All controlled substances require proper logging, secure storage, and DEA oversight.

---

### Q4.6
**Diazepam (Valium) is classified under which DEA controlled substance schedule?**
- ✓ Schedule IV
- ✗ Schedule II
- ✗ Schedule III
- ✗ It is not a controlled substance

**Explanation:** Diazepam is Schedule IV, indicating low potential for abuse and dependence relative to Schedule III drugs. Other Schedule IV veterinary drugs include midazolam, butorphanol, phenobarbital, and tramadol.

---

### Q4.7
**Hydromorphone and fentanyl are classified under which DEA schedule?**
- ✓ Schedule II — high potential for abuse and severe dependence
- ✗ Schedule III
- ✗ Schedule IV
- ✗ Schedule V

**Explanation:** Schedule II drugs have high abuse potential and include full-mu opioid agonists like hydromorphone, fentanyl, morphine, and oxymorphone. They require the highest level of record-keeping and security.

---

### Q4.8
**Which drug class do carprofen (Rimadyl), meloxicam (Metacam), and deracoxib (Deramaxx) belong to?**
- ✓ Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
- ✗ Opioid analgesics
- ✗ Corticosteroids
- ✗ Antibiotics

**Explanation:** These are all NSAIDs commonly used for pain and inflammation in dogs. They work by inhibiting cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes. Key concerns include GI ulceration, renal effects, and hepatotoxicity. They should NOT be used concurrently with corticosteroids.

---

### Q4.9
**Meloxicam should be used with extreme caution (or avoided entirely) in which species?**
- ✓ Cats — especially with repeated dosing, due to high risk of acute kidney injury
- ✗ Dogs — they are highly sensitive to all NSAIDs
- ✗ Horses — all NSAIDs are contraindicated in equines
- ✗ Rabbits — there are no safe pain medications for this species

**Explanation:** While meloxicam has an FDA-approved single injectable dose for cats, repeated oral dosing carries significant risk of nephrotoxicity. The FDA has specifically warned against repeated meloxicam use in cats.

---

### Q4.10
**A 15 kg dog is 8% dehydrated. How much fluid (in mL) is needed to correct the deficit?**
- ✓ 1,200 mL
- ✗ 120 mL
- ✗ 800 mL
- ✗ 150 mL

**Explanation:** Dehydration deficit = Body weight (kg) × % dehydration × 1000 = 15 × 0.08 × 1000 = 1,200 mL. This deficit is typically replaced over 12-24 hours, added to the maintenance fluid rate.

---

### Q4.11
**What is the standard dose of epinephrine for CPR in dogs and cats (IV/IO route)?**
- ✓ 0.01 mg/kg of 1:10,000 dilution (low dose)
- ✗ 1 mg/kg of 1:1,000 dilution
- ✗ 0.1 mg/kg IV undiluted
- ✗ 0.5 mg/kg subcutaneously

**Explanation:** Per RECOVER CPR guidelines, epinephrine is given at 0.01 mg/kg IV/IO using the 1:10,000 dilution (= 0.1 mL/kg). If given endotracheally, the dose is 10x higher (0.1 mg/kg of 1:1,000). Repeated every 3-5 minutes during CPR.

---

### Q4.12
**Atropine during CPR is dosed at:**
- ✓ 0.02–0.04 mg/kg IV/IO
- ✗ 0.5 mg/kg IV
- ✗ 2 mg/kg IM
- ✗ 0.001 mg/kg SQ

**Explanation:** Atropine at 0.02-0.04 mg/kg IV/IO is used during CPR for asystole or pulseless electrical activity (PEA) with a rate <60 bpm. It may be repeated every 3-5 minutes. The endotracheal dose is typically doubled.

---

## CATEGORY 5: LABORATORY SKILLS

### Q5.1
**Which blood draw site is preferred when a large-volume blood sample is needed from a dog or cat?**
- ✓ Jugular vein
- ✗ Cephalic vein
- ✗ Lateral saphenous vein
- ✗ Sublingual vein

**Explanation:** The jugular vein is the largest easily accessible peripheral vessel and yields large-volume samples with minimal hemolysis. It is the preferred site for routine CBCs, chemistry panels, and any time multiple tubes are needed.

---

### Q5.2
**In cats, the preferred hindlimb blood draw site is the:**
- ✓ Medial saphenous (femoral) vein — located on the inside of the thigh
- ✗ Lateral saphenous vein — located on the outside of the hindlimb
- ✗ Dorsal pedal vein — located on top of the foot
- ✗ Coccygeal (tail) vein

**Explanation:** In cats, the medial saphenous vein on the medial aspect of the hindlimb is preferred. In dogs, by contrast, the lateral saphenous on the outside of the hindlimb is used. This is a commonly tested species difference.

---

### Q5.3
**What is the normal PCV (packed cell volume/hematocrit) range for a dog?**
- ✓ 37–55%
- ✗ 20–30%
- ✗ 60–75%
- ✗ 10–15%

**Explanation:** Normal canine PCV is 37-55%. Values below 37% indicate anemia, while values above 55% may indicate dehydration or polycythemia. For cats, normal PCV is 30-45%.

---

### Q5.4
**A cat has a PCV of 18%. This indicates:**
- ✓ Severe anemia — likely requires transfusion
- ✗ Normal for a cat
- ✗ Polycythemia (too many red blood cells)
- ✗ Dehydration

**Explanation:** Normal feline PCV is 30-45%. A PCV of 18% is severely anemic. Clinical signs will include pale mucous membranes, tachycardia, weakness, and potentially collapse. A blood transfusion is often needed when PCV drops below 15-20%.

---

### Q5.5
**Normal urine specific gravity (USG) for a cat is:**
- ✓ 1.035–1.065
- ✗ 1.001–1.008
- ✗ 1.010–1.015
- ✗ 1.070–1.090

**Explanation:** Cats concentrate their urine well, with normal USG of 1.035-1.065. Dogs have a wider normal range of 1.015-1.045. Isosthenuria (USG 1.008-1.012) indicates the kidneys are not concentrating or diluting urine and suggests renal tubular dysfunction.

---

### Q5.6
**On fecal flotation, you observe barrel-shaped eggs with prominent bipolar plugs (resembling a lemon or football). These are most likely:**
- ✓ Whipworm (Trichuris vulpis) eggs
- ✗ Roundworm (Toxocara) eggs
- ✗ Hookworm (Ancylostoma) eggs
- ✗ Coccidia (Cystoisospora) oocysts

**Explanation:** Whipworm eggs are barrel-shaped with characteristic bipolar plugs at each end. Roundworm eggs are spherical with a thick, rough (mammillated) shell. Hookworm eggs are oval with a thin smooth shell and internal morula. Coccidia oocysts are small and round.

---

### Q5.7
**On fecal flotation, you see large (70–90 µm), round eggs with a thick, rough (pitted/mammillated), dark-brown shell. These are most likely:**
- ✓ Roundworm (Toxocara spp.) eggs
- ✗ Hookworm eggs
- ✗ Whipworm eggs
- ✗ Tapeworm eggs

**Explanation:** Toxocara (roundworm) eggs are large, spherical, with a distinctive thick, coarsely mammillated (pitted) outer shell that appears brown/orange. They contain a single dark zygote filling most of the egg.

---

### Q5.8
**Struvite crystals in urine are most commonly associated with which urine pH?**
- ✓ Alkaline pH (>7.0)
- ✗ Strongly acidic pH (<5.0)
- ✗ Neutral pH only (exactly 7.0)
- ✗ pH has no effect on crystal formation

**Explanation:** Struvite (magnesium ammonium phosphate) crystals form in alkaline to neutral urine. They are commonly associated with urinary tract infections in dogs (urease-producing bacteria raise pH). Calcium oxalate crystals, by contrast, form in acidic to neutral urine.

---

### Q5.9
**What blood type are the majority of domestic cats?**
- ✓ Type A
- ✗ Type B
- ✗ Type AB
- ✗ Type O

**Explanation:** Approximately 90-99% of domestic shorthair and longhair cats are Type A (prevalence varies by breed and region). Certain purebred cats (British Shorthair, Devon Rex, Cornish Rex) have higher rates of Type B. Type AB is very rare.

---

### Q5.10
**Why is blood typing and crossmatching critically important BEFORE the first transfusion in cats (unlike dogs)?**

- ✗ Cats develop antibodies only after their second transfusion, similar to dogs
- ✗ Blood typing is only important for Type AB cats

**Explanation:** Unlike dogs, cats have pre-existing (naturally occurring) antibodies against other blood types. A Type B cat given Type A blood can have a rapidly fatal hemolytic reaction — even a single mL of mismatched blood. Dogs generally tolerate a first mismatched transfusion but develop antibodies afterward.

**Metzenbaum scissors are used primarily for:**

- ✗ Cutting heavy connective tissue like fascia
- ✗ Cutting suture material
- ✗ Cutting bandage material

**Explanation:** Metzenbaum scissors ("Metz") have long handles with short, delicate blades, making them ideal for gentle tissue dissection. Mayo scissors are heavier and used for cutting tough tissue and sutures. Bandage scissors have a blunt tip for safe bandage removal.

### Q6.2
**Rochester-Carmalt hemostats are characterized by:**

- ✗ Completely smooth jaws with no serrations
- ✗ Short, delicate jaws identical to mosquito hemostats

**Explanation:** Rochester-Carmalt hemostats have distinctive parallel longitudinal grooves along most of the jaw length, with a criss-cross (checkerboard) pattern at the tip. ### Q6.3
**What temperature and duration are standard for autoclaving wrapped surgical instrument packs?**

- ✗ 100°C (212°F) at normal pressure for 10 minutes

**Explanation:** Standard steam autoclave sterilization uses 121°C (250°F) at 15 psi for at least 30 minutes for wrapped packs. A "flash" cycle at 134°C (273°F) can be used for 3-10 minutes for unwrapped instruments in emergencies, but is not ideal.

- ✗ Guarantees that all instruments inside are sterile
- ✗ Indicates the pack has exceeded its expiration date

### Q6.5
**When putting on sterile surgical gloves using the closed-gloving technique, your hands should:**

- ✗ Be fully extended out of the gown sleeves before gloving
- ✗ Be bare (gown not yet worn) during gloving
- ✗ Touch the outside of the glove package to orient the gloves

**Explanation:** In closed gloving, the hands stay within the gown cuffs, and the gloves are manipulated over the cuff without any bare skin exposure. This is the preferred method for maintaining sterility. Open gloving is used when a gown is not worn.

### Q6.6
**PDS (polydioxanone) suture is classified as:**

- ✗ Natural, braided, absorbable
- ✗ Synthetic, braided, non-absorbable
- ✗ Natural, monofilament, non-absorbable

**Explanation:** PDS is a synthetic monofilament absorbable suture with a long absorption time (~180 days). It is commonly used for body wall closure (linea alba), internal tissues, and areas where prolonged tensile strength is needed.

### Q6.7
**Nylon suture (Ethilon) is classified as:**
- ✓ Synthetic, monofilament, non-absorbable

- ✗ Natural, monofilament, absorbable

**Explanation:** Nylon (Ethilon) is a synthetic non-absorbable monofilament suture commonly used for skin closure. Its monofilament structure provides smooth tissue passage and resists infection wicking. Skin sutures are removed 10-14 days post-surgery.

### Q6.8
**Which suture material is preferred in contaminated or infected wounds?**

**Explanation:** Monofilament sutures have a smooth surface that resists bacterial colonization. Braided sutures (silk, Vicryl) have interstices between fibers that can harbor bacteria, wicking infection deeper into tissue. This makes monofilament the clear choice in contaminated environments.

- ✗ A single continuous running thread closes the entire incision

- ✗ Sternal recumbency (on the chest)

## CATEGORY 7: RADIOLOGY & IMAGING

### Q7.1
**What does the acronym ALARA stand for in radiation safety?**
- ✓ As Low As Reasonably Achievable
- ✗ Always Lead-Apron Required for All
- ✗ Automatic Limitation And Radiation Assessment
- ✗ Annual Limit of Absorbed Radiation Allowance

**Explanation:** ALARA is the guiding principle of radiation safety: minimize exposure through reduced time near the source, maximized distance from the beam, and proper shielding (lead aprons, gloves, thyroid shields).

---

### Q7.2
**In a ventrodorsal (VD) radiographic view, the patient is positioned:**
- ✓ On its back (dorsal recumbency) with the X-ray beam traveling from ventral to dorsal
- ✗ On its stomach (sternal recumbency) with the beam going dorsal to ventral
- ✗ On its side (lateral recumbency)
- ✗ Standing on all four legs

**Explanation:** The naming convention describes beam direction: Ventro(first)-Dorsal(second) means the beam enters ventrally and exits dorsally, so the patient must be on its back. The cassette/detector is under the patient's dorsum.

---

### Q7.3
**In a dorsoventral (DV) radiographic view, the patient is positioned:**
- ✓ On its stomach (sternal recumbency) with the beam traveling from dorsal to ventral
- ✗ On its back (dorsal recumbency)
- ✗ On its right side
- ✗ Hanging vertically by the forelimbs

**Explanation:** Dorso(first)-Ventral(second) = beam enters dorsally and exits ventrally. The patient lies on its chest/abdomen. DV is often preferred for dyspneic (difficulty breathing) patients because sternal recumbency is less stressful than lying on their back.

---

### Q7.4
**Why is the DV view often preferred over the VD view for a patient with respiratory distress?**
- ✓ Sternal recumbency (DV) is less stressful and allows easier breathing than dorsal recumbency (VD)
- ✗ DV gives a better view of the heart
- ✗ VD requires the patient to be sedated, while DV does not
- ✗ DV uses less radiation than VD

**Explanation:** A dyspneic patient placed on its back (VD) may decompensate because the abdominal organs compress the lungs. Sternal recumbency (DV) is a more natural posture that maintains better lung ventilation and is safer for compromised patients.

---

### Q7.5
**The three principles of radiation protection are:**
- ✓ Time, distance, and shielding
- ✗ Speed, accuracy, and monitoring
- ✗ Collimation, filtration, and exposure
- ✗ kVp, mA, and time

**Explanation:** Minimize time of exposure, maximize distance from the radiation source, and use shielding (lead aprons, gloves, thyroid shields, lead-lined walls). These three principles implement the ALARA concept.

---

### Q7.6
**Dosimetry badges worn by veterinary staff are used to:**
- ✓ Monitor cumulative radiation exposure over time
- ✗ Protect the wearer from radiation
- ✗ Determine the correct exposure settings for each patient
- ✗ Detect radioactive contamination on clothing

**Explanation:** Dosimetry badges (film badges or OSL dosimeters) are personal monitoring devices that record radiation exposure. They are worn at the collar level, outside the lead apron, and are collected and analyzed regularly (usually monthly or quarterly) to ensure staff stay within safe exposure limits.

---

### Q7.7
**When manually restraining a patient during radiography is absolutely unavoidable, personnel must:**
- ✓ Wear lead aprons, gloves, and thyroid shields, and keep all body parts out of the primary beam
- ✗ Simply stand as far from the machine as possible without any PPE
- ✗ Only wear lead gloves — aprons are not needed
- ✗ Hold their breath to reduce radiation absorption

**Explanation:** If sedation/chemical restraint or positioning aids cannot be used and manual restraint is unavoidable, all exposed personnel must wear full lead PPE and ensure NO body part enters the primary beam. Chemical restraint or positioning devices should be used whenever possible.

---

### Q7.8
**Collimation of the X-ray beam is important because it:**
- ✓ Reduces the area of tissue exposed to radiation and improves image quality by reducing scatter
- ✗ Increases the amount of radiation needed for a diagnostic image
- ✗ Eliminates the need for lead aprons
- ✗ Only affects the brightness of the image, not safety

**Explanation:** Collimation (restricting the beam to only the area of interest) reduces patient exposure, reduces scatter radiation (improving contrast), and reduces staff exposure. It is a key component of the ALARA principle.

---

### Q7.9
**A right lateral radiographic view means:**
- ✓ The patient's right side is against the cassette/table (right lateral recumbency), and the beam enters from the left
- ✗ The patient's left side is against the table, and the beam enters from the right
- ✗ The beam enters from the patient's right side
- ✗ The patient is standing and the beam enters from the right

**Explanation:** In radiology naming convention, the side listed is the side DOWN (closest to the film/detector). Right lateral = right side down. The beam enters from the opposite (left) side, passes through the patient, and hits the film on the right.

---

### Q7.10
**Which of the following is NOT standard personal protective equipment (PPE) for radiography?**
- ✓ Lead-lined face mask — not standard radiographic PPE
- ✗ Lead apron
- ✗ Lead gloves
- ✗ Thyroid shield

**Explanation:** Standard radiographic PPE includes lead aprons, lead gloves (when hands may be near the beam), and thyroid shields. Lead face masks are not standard equipment. Lead glasses may be used in facilities with high caseloads (e.g., fluoroscopy).

## CATEGORY 8: EMERGENCY & CRITICAL CARE

### Q8.1
**Per RECOVER CPR guidelines, what is the recommended chest compression rate for dogs and cats?**
- ✓ 100–120 compressions per minute
- ✗ 60 compressions per minute
- ✗ 30 compressions per minute
- ✗ 200 compressions per minute

**Explanation:** RECOVER (REassessment Campaign On VEterinary Resuscitation) recommends 100-120 compressions per minute for both dogs and cats. Compressions should achieve 1/3 to 1/2 the width of the chest. This rate is the same as human AHA guidelines.

---

### Q8.2
**What is the compression-to-ventilation ratio for an un-intubated patient during CPR?**
- ✓ 30:2 (30 compressions, then 2 breaths)
- ✗ 15:2
- ✗ 5:1
- ✗ Continuous compressions with no ventilation

**Explanation:** For un-intubated patients, the ratio is 30:2. Once the patient is intubated, compressions become continuous (uninterrupted) while ventilation is provided asynchronously at 10 breaths per minute.

---

### Q8.3
**During CPR on an intubated patient, how often should ventilations be given?**
- ✓ 10 breaths per minute (one breath every 6 seconds), asynchronous with compressions
- ✗ 30 breaths per minute
- ✗ Only when compressions are paused
- ✗ 2 breaths after every 30 compressions

**Explanation:** Once intubated, ventilation and compressions are decoupled (asynchronous). Provide 10 breaths/min while maintaining continuous chest compressions. This maximizes coronary perfusion by avoiding interruptions.

---

### Q8.4
**The classic signs of shock in a dog include all of the following EXCEPT:**
- ✓ Bradycardia and hypertension — these are NOT typical of early/compensatory shock in dogs
- ✗ Tachycardia (elevated heart rate)
- ✗ Pale or muddy mucous membranes
- ✗ Prolonged capillary refill time (CRT > 2 seconds)

**Explanation:** Early compensatory shock in dogs presents with tachycardia, weak/bounding pulses, pale mucous membranes, prolonged CRT, and hypotension. Cats may present differently — they often show bradycardia and hypothermia in decompensated shock.

---

### Q8.5
**A dog ingests dark chocolate. The toxic component of greatest concern is:**
- ✓ Theobromine (methylxanthine)
- ✗ Sucrose (sugar)
- ✗ Cocoa butter (fat)
- ✗ Caffeine (though present, theobromine is the primary toxin)

**Explanation:** Theobromine is the primary toxic methylxanthine in chocolate for dogs. Dark/baker's chocolate has the highest concentration (~130-450 mg/oz). Mild signs occur at ~20 mg/kg, cardiac effects at ~40-50 mg/kg, and seizures at >60 mg/kg theobromine.

---

### Q8.6
**Xylitol (birch sugar) ingestion in dogs can cause which two major clinical syndromes?**
- ✓ Hypoglycemia (rapid insulin release) and hepatic (liver) failure
- ✗ Hyperglycemia and kidney failure
- ✗ Respiratory depression and cardiac arrest
- ✗ Hemolytic anemia and coagulopathy

**Explanation:** Xylitol causes a massive insulin release in dogs, leading to severe hypoglycemia within 30-60 minutes. At higher doses (>0.5 g/kg), it can cause acute hepatic necrosis within 12-72 hours. Blood glucose monitoring and IV dextrose are essential.

---

### Q8.7
**Which plant is acutely nephrotoxic (causes kidney failure) in cats even with very small exposures, including contact with pollen?**
- ✓ Lilies (Lilium and Hemerocallis species — Easter lily, tiger lily, daylily, etc.)
- ✗ Poinsettia
- ✗ Aloe vera
- ✗ Spider plant

**Explanation:** True lilies (Lilium spp.) and daylilies (Hemerocallis spp.) are profoundly nephrotoxic to cats. ALL parts of the plant are toxic, including pollen and vase water. Even small exposures can cause fatal acute kidney injury within 24-72 hours without aggressive IV fluid therapy.

---

### Q8.8
**Grapes and raisins are toxic to dogs primarily because they cause:**
- ✓ Acute kidney injury (nephrotoxicity) — the specific toxic agent is still unknown
- ✗ Liver failure
- ✗ Severe hemolytic anemia
- ✗ Pancreatitis

**Explanation:** Grapes, raisins, and currants can cause acute kidney injury in dogs. The toxic principle remains unidentified (recent research suggests tartaric acid may be involved). Even small amounts can be toxic, and there is no known safe dose. Treatment includes decontamination and aggressive IV fluid therapy.

---

### Q8.9
**A Robert Jones bandage is primarily used for:**
- ✓ Temporary immobilization and swelling control of fractures or injuries of the distal limb (below the elbow or stifle)
- ✗ Covering wounds on the head and neck
- ✗ Permanent fracture fixation as an alternative to surgery
- ✗ Wrapping the thorax after rib fractures

**Explanation:** The Robert Jones bandage is a heavily padded bandage that provides temporary stabilization of distal limb injuries, reduces swelling through compression, and immobilizes the limb until definitive treatment (surgery) can be performed. It is NOT appropriate for proximal limb (femur/humerus) fractures.

---

### Q8.10
**A properly applied bandage has three layers. From innermost to outermost, they are:**

- ✗ Padded layer → contact layer → outer layer
- ✗ Tape → gauze → cotton
- ✗ Outer wrap → absorbent layer → wound dressing

**Explanation:** The contact layer touches the wound (nonadherent or adherent depending on wound stage). The secondary layer provides padding and absorbs exudate. The tertiary layer protects and holds everything in place (conforming gauze, Vetrap/elastic wrap).

### Q8.11
**After applying a distal limb bandage, which area should be left exposed to monitor for vascular compromise?**
- ✓ The toes (digits) — checked for swelling, coldness, and discoloration
- ✗ The elbow joint
- ✗ The inguinal region
- ✗ Nothing needs to be left exposed

**Explanation:** The two middle toes (digits 3 and 4) are typically left exposed so they can be monitored for swelling, coldness, pain, and discoloration. These signs indicate the bandage may be too tight and compromising blood flow.

---

### Q8.12
**A cat presents in decompensated shock. Unlike dogs, cats in decompensatory shock classically present with:**
- ✓ Bradycardia and hypothermia
- ✗ Tachycardia and fever
- ✗ Hypertension and bounding pulses
- ✗ Bright red mucous membranes and rapid CRT

**Explanation:** Cats in decompensated shock often present with bradycardia (not tachycardia), hypothermia, and weak pulses — the opposite of the classic "dog shock" presentation. This is a critical species difference that affects treatment decisions.

## CATEGORY 9: RESTRAINT & HANDLING

### Q9.1
**The "burrito wrap" (towel wrap) is a fear-free restraint technique primarily used for:**
- ✓ Cats — provides secure restraint while minimizing stress and the need for scruffing
- ✗ Large-breed dogs
- ✗ Horses during hoof trimming
- ✗ Reptiles during feeding

**Explanation:** The burrito wrap involves placing the cat on a large towel, then wrapping the towel snugly around the body, leaving only the head exposed (or exposing a specific limb for procedures). It provides secure restraint while giving the cat a sense of being hidden and protected.

### Q9.2
**Modern fear-free veterinary practice recommends AGAINST routine scruffing of cats because:**

- ✗ It has no effect on cats at all

**Explanation:** While scruffing was historically standard practice, current fear-free and AAFP guidelines recognize it causes distress in adult cats. Alternative techniques include towel wraps, gentle shoulder pressure, minimal restraint, and distraction with treats. Scruffing may be acceptable only as a last resort in emergencies.

### Q9.3
**When restraining a dog for a cephalic blood draw, the restrainer should:**

- ✗ Hold the dog's muzzle closed with both hands

**Explanation:** For cephalic venipuncture, the restrainer sits or stands at the dog's head, wraps one arm around the dog's body/neck for stability, and uses the other hand to occlude the cephalic vein just above the elbow while extending the forelimb and rotating the vein to the lateral surface.

### Q9.4
**The lateral saphenous vein in dogs is accessed with the dog in which position?**
- ✓ Lateral recumbency (lying on the side), with the upper hindlimb held out of the way
- ✗ Dorsal recumbency
- ✗ Standing with the hindlimb extended backward
- ✗ Sternal recumbency

**Explanation:** The dog is placed in lateral recumbency. The person restraining holds the upper hindlimb out of the way and occludes the lateral saphenous vein on the down leg by applying pressure above the stifle/hock on the lateral aspect.

### Q9.5
**Which of the following is a key principle of Fear-Free veterinary handling?**
- ✓ Use the minimum restraint necessary to safely perform the procedure, and employ treats, pheromones, and gentle handling
- ✗ Always use the maximum restraint available to prevent any chance of being bitten
- ✗ Complete the procedure as quickly as possible, regardless of the animal's stress level
- ✗ Sedate every patient before any examination

**Explanation:** Fear-Free methodology emphasizes minimal restraint, positive experiences (treats, pheromones like Feliway/Adaptil), gentle handling, and reading body language. The goal is to reduce fear, anxiety, and stress (FAS) at every veterinary visit.

### Q9.6

### Q9.8

## CATEGORY 10: MEDICAL NURSING & PATIENT CARE

### Q10.1
**What is the normal rectal temperature range for both dogs and cats?**
- ✓ 100.0–102.5°F (37.8–39.2°C)
- ✗ 97.0–98.6°F (36.1–37.0°C) — this is the human normal range
- ✗ 104.0–106.0°F (40.0–41.1°C)
- ✗ 95.0–97.0°F (35.0–36.1°C)

**Explanation:** Dogs and cats share a similar normal temperature range of 100.0-102.5°F. Temperatures above 103°F may indicate fever or hyperthermia, while temperatures below 99°F may indicate hypothermia, particularly concerning under anesthesia.

---

### Q10.2
**The normal resting heart rate for a cat is:**
- ✓ 140–220 beats per minute
- ✗ 60–100 beats per minute (this is the human range)
- ✗ 70–120 beats per minute (this is more typical of a large dog)
- ✗ 300–400 beats per minute

**Explanation:** Cats have a higher resting heart rate (140-220 bpm) compared to dogs (70-160 bpm, varying by size). Small breed dogs and puppies trend toward the higher end, while giant breeds trend lower.

---

### Q10.3
**On the 1–9 Body Condition Score (BCS) scale, what score represents an ideal body condition?**
- ✓ 4–5 (with 5 being ideal)
- ✗ 1 (underweight is ideal)
- ✗ 7 (slightly overweight is considered healthy)
- ✗ 9 (maximum body condition)

**Explanation:** On the 1-9 BCS scale (WSAVA/Purina), 4-5 is ideal. The ribs should be easily palpable with slight fat covering, there should be a visible waist from above, and an abdominal tuck from the side. A score of 1 = emaciated, 9 = morbidly obese.

---

### Q10.4
**IV catheters in dogs and cats should generally be replaced or reassessed every:**
- ✓ 72 hours (some protocols allow up to 96 hours)
- ✗ 24 hours
- ✗ 7 days
- ✗ They never need replacement if the site looks clean

**Explanation:** Standard of care is to replace peripheral IV catheters every 72-96 hours to reduce the risk of phlebitis, thrombosis, and catheter-related infection. The site should be inspected every 4-6 hours for swelling, heat, redness, pain, or discharge.

---

### Q10.5
**What gauge IV catheter is most appropriate for a 4 kg cat?**
- ✓ 22 gauge
- ✗ 14 gauge (this is appropriate for a large dog needing rapid fluid delivery)
- ✗ 18 gauge (typically too large for most cats)
- ✗ 8 gauge (this size does not exist for peripheral IV catheters)

**Explanation:** Cats and small dogs typically receive 22-24 gauge catheters due to their small vein size. Medium dogs use 20 gauge, large dogs use 18-20 gauge. Always use the smallest gauge appropriate for the clinical need to minimize vessel trauma.

---

### Q10.6
**What solution should be used to flush a peripheral IV catheter to maintain patency?**
- ✓ Sterile 0.9% saline (normal saline), 1–3 mL
- ✗ Sterile water for injection
- ✗ Lactated Ringer's solution straight from the fluid bag
- ✗ Heparinized saline at full anticoagulant doses

**Explanation:** Sterile 0.9% NaCl is the standard flush solution. Sterile water is hypotonic and can cause hemolysis. While dilute heparin saline was historically used, recent evidence supports plain saline as equally effective for maintaining patency. Flush every 6-8 hours if the catheter is not in continuous use.

---

### Q10.7
**The Glasgow Composite Measure Pain Scale - Short Form (CMPS-SF) is validated for use in:**
- ✓ Dogs — it assesses acute pain through behavioral observations
- ✗ Cats — it uses the Feline Grimace Scale
- ✗ Horses only
- ✗ All mammalian species equally

**Explanation:** The Glasgow CMPS-SF was specifically developed and validated for dogs. It evaluates vocalization, attention to wound, mobility, response to touch, demeanor, and posture. Scores ≥6/24 (or ≥5/20 without mobility) indicate the patient likely needs additional analgesia. For cats, the Feline Grimace Scale is used.

---

### Q10.8
**The Feline Grimace Scale assesses pain in cats by evaluating:**
- ✓ Five facial action units: ear position, orbital tightening, muzzle tension, whisker position, and head position
- ✗ Vocalization volume and frequency only
- ✗ Heart rate and respiratory rate only
- ✗ Appetite and litter box usage only

**Explanation:** The Feline Grimace Scale scores 5 facial action units (0-2 each, max 10). A score ≥4/10 suggests the cat needs analgesic intervention. It is based on subtle facial changes and requires training to use effectively but is a validated, practical tool.

---

### Q10.9
**Normal capillary refill time (CRT) in a dog or cat is:**
- ✓ Less than 2 seconds
- ✗ 4–5 seconds
- ✗ Less than 0.5 seconds (flash refill indicates vasodilation)
- ✗ 10 seconds

**Explanation:** Normal CRT is <2 seconds when tested by pressing on the gingival mucosa. Prolonged CRT (>2 seconds) suggests poor perfusion (dehydration, shock, cardiovascular disease). A "flash" CRT (<1 second) can indicate vasodilation or early distributive shock.

---

### Q10.10
**Normal mucous membrane color in a healthy dog or cat is:**
- ✓ Pink (salmon pink)
- ✗ White/pale (indicates anemia or poor perfusion)
- ✗ Brick red/injected (indicates toxemia, sepsis, or heat stroke)
- ✗ Blue/cyanotic (indicates severe hypoxemia)

**Explanation:** Healthy mucous membranes are pink. Pale = anemia or vasoconstriction. Brick red = vasodilation (early shock, sepsis, heat stroke). Yellow (icteric) = liver disease or hemolysis. Blue (cyanotic) = severe hypoxemia. Muddy/gray = poor perfusion and imminent decompensation.

## CATEGORY 11: COMMUNICATION & CLIENT EDUCATION

### Q11.1
**When providing post-dental discharge instructions to a client, which of the following should be included?**
- ✓ Feed soft food for 7-14 days, monitor for excessive bleeding or swelling, administer prescribed pain medication, and schedule a recheck if extractions were performed
- ✗ Resume regular hard kibble and chew toys immediately
- ✗ Brush the teeth vigorously that same evening to remove remaining plaque
- ✗ No follow-up care is needed after dental procedures

**Explanation:** Post-dental care includes soft food to protect healing extraction sites, pain management, monitoring for complications (bleeding, swelling, inappetence), and withholding hard chew toys. Clients should be given written discharge instructions and a recheck appointment if indicated.

### Q11.2
**When a client asks, "What does it mean that my dog's bloodwork shows elevated liver values?", the most appropriate vet tech response is:**
- ✓ "Elevated liver values can indicate that the liver is stressed or inflamed. Dr. [Vet] will discuss the specific results, what they might mean for [pet name], and recommend any next steps."
- ✗ "Your dog has liver failure and will need a liver transplant."
- ✗ "I'm not allowed to talk about test results at all — you'll have to wait for the doctor."
- ✗ "Don't worry about it. Liver values go up all the time. It's nothing."

**Explanation:** Vet techs should provide general, accurate information within their scope without making diagnoses. Referring to the veterinarian for specific interpretation and treatment planning is appropriate. Dismissing concerns or making dramatic diagnoses are both inappropriate.

### Q11.3
**During a euthanasia appointment, a key role of the veterinary technician is to:**
- ✓ Provide emotional support to the client, explain what to expect during the process, ensure a calm and private environment, and assist the veterinarian with the procedure
- ✗ Discourage the client from being present during the procedure
- ✗ Rush the process to minimize the clinic's emotional burden
- ✗ Avoid discussing the procedure to prevent upsetting the client

**Explanation:** Vet techs play a crucial supportive role during euthanasia: preparing a comfortable space, explaining the process (sedation, injection, possible reflexes), providing tissues and time, answering questions, and supporting grieving clients. Many practices also provide aftercare information and grief resources.

### Q11.4
**A client calls to say their indoor cat just ate part of a lily plant. The vet tech should:**
- ✓ Advise the client to bring the cat in immediately — lily ingestion is a life-threatening emergency in cats
- ✗ Tell the client to monitor at home for 48 hours and call back if symptoms develop
- ✗ Recommend inducing vomiting at home with hydrogen peroxide
- ✗ Reassure the client that lilies are only mildly toxic and the cat will be fine

**Explanation:** Lily toxicity in cats is a TRUE EMERGENCY. Any exposure (ingestion of petals, leaves, pollen, or even vase water) can cause fatal acute kidney injury. Immediate veterinary care with aggressive IV fluids and decontamination is essential. Waiting for symptoms dramatically worsens prognosis.

### Q11.5
**When discharging a patient after surgery, written discharge instructions should include:**
- ✓ Activity restrictions, medication instructions, incision monitoring, feeding guidelines, signs of complications to watch for, and when to return for recheck/suture removal
- ✗ Only the date for suture removal — no other information is needed
- ✗ A verbal summary only — written instructions are unnecessary
- ✗ Instructions to let the pet lick the incision as this promotes healing

**Explanation:** Comprehensive written discharge instructions ensure client compliance and improve patient outcomes. They should cover exercise restriction, E-collar use, medication details (drug, dose, frequency, duration), incision care, diet modifications, and emergency contact information.

### Q11.6
**A client reports that their dog is drinking and urinating much more than usual. The medical terms for these findings are:**

- ✗ Oliguria (reduced urination) and bradypnea (slow breathing)
- ✗ Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing) and dysuria (painful urination)

**Explanation:** Polydipsia/polyuria (PD/PU) is one of the most common owner complaints and can indicate diabetes mellitus, kidney disease, Cushing's disease, hyperthyroidism (cats), pyometra, or other conditions. Accurately using medical terminology helps communicate efficiently with the veterinary team.

## CATEGORY 12: VETERINARY MEDICAL TERMINOLOGY

### Q12.1
**The suffix "-ectomy" means:**
- ✓ Surgical removal/excision
- ✗ Inflammation
- ✗ Surgical repair
- ✗ Visual examination

**Explanation:** -ectomy = removal (e.g., ovariohysterectomy = removal of ovaries and uterus, splenectomy = removal of spleen). Compare with: -itis = inflammation, -plasty = surgical repair, -scopy = visual examination.

---

### Q12.2
**The term "bradycardia" means:**
- ✓ Abnormally slow heart rate
- ✗ Abnormally fast heart rate (that would be tachycardia)
- ✗ Irregular heart rhythm
- ✗ Absence of heartbeat

**Explanation:** Brady- = slow, -cardia = heart. Bradycardia = slow heart rate. Tachy- = fast (tachycardia). Dys- = abnormal/difficult. A- = without (asystole = no cardiac electrical activity).

---

### Q12.3
**The directional term "caudal" means:**
- ✓ Toward the tail
- ✗ Toward the head (that would be cranial)
- ✗ Toward the belly (that would be ventral)
- ✗ Toward the spine (that would be dorsal)

**Explanation:** Caudal = toward the tail. Cranial = toward the head. Dorsal = toward the back/spine. Ventral = toward the belly. These directional terms are used throughout the body except on the head (where rostral replaces cranial) and below the carpus/tarsus (where palmar/plantar/dorsal are used).

---

### Q12.4
**The term "rostral" means:**
- ✓ Toward the nose — used specifically on the head
- ✗ Toward the tail
- ✗ Toward the spine
- ✗ Away from the midline

**Explanation:** Rostral is used instead of "cranial" when describing directions on the head itself. Since the entire head is already the "cranial" part of the body, "rostral" specifies direction toward the nose/tip of the muzzle.

---

### Q12.5
**The term "proximal" means:**
- ✓ Closer to the point of attachment or origin (toward the body)
- ✗ Farther from the point of attachment (that would be distal)
- ✗ On the surface of the skin
- ✗ Deep within the body

**Explanation:** Proximal = closer to the body/point of attachment. Distal = farther away. These terms are primarily used for limbs and appendages. Example: The elbow is proximal to the carpus (wrist); the toes are the most distal part of the limb.

---

### Q12.6
**The abbreviation "SID" (or "q24h") on a prescription means:**
- ✓ Once daily
- ✗ Twice daily (that would be BID)
- ✗ Three times daily (that would be TID)
- ✗ Four times daily (that would be QID)

**Explanation:** SID = semel in die (once daily), also written as q24h. BID = bis in die (twice daily/q12h). TID = ter in die (three times daily/q8h). QID = quarter in die (four times daily/q6h). Note: SID is veterinary-specific; human medicine uses "QD."

---

### Q12.7
**The abbreviation "PO" on a prescription means:**
- ✓ Per os — by mouth (oral administration)
- ✗ Per oculum — applied to the eye
- ✗ Post-operative
- ✗ Physician's order

**Explanation:** PO = per os = by mouth. This is one of the most common prescription abbreviations. Other routes: SQ/SC = subcutaneous, IM = intramuscular, IV = intravenous, IN = intranasal, PR = per rectum.

---

### Q12.8
**The medical term for "inflammation of the liver" is:**
- ✓ Hepatitis (hepat/o = liver, -itis = inflammation)
- ✗ Nephritis (nephr/o = kidney)
- ✗ Hepatoma (hepat/o = liver, -oma = tumor)
- ✗ Gastritis (gastr/o = stomach)

**Explanation:** Combining the root "hepat/o" (liver) with the suffix "-itis" (inflammation) gives "hepatitis." This follows the standard medical terminology construction pattern: root + suffix.

---

### Q12.9
**The medical term for "condition of low blood sugar" is:**
- ✓ Hypoglycemia (hypo- = low, glyc/o = sugar, -emia = blood condition)
- ✗ Hyperglycemia (hyper- = high)
- ✗ Hypocalcemia (calc/o = calcium)
- ✗ Glycosuria (glyc/o = sugar, -uria = in the urine)

**Explanation:** Breaking down: hypo- (below/low) + glyc/o (sugar/glucose) + -emia (blood condition) = low blood sugar. Hyperglycemia would be high blood sugar. Glycosuria = sugar in the urine.

---

### Q12.10
**The abbreviation "OD" refers to:**
- ✓ Right eye (oculus dexter)
- ✗ Left eye (that would be OS — oculus sinister)
- ✗ Both eyes (that would be OU — oculus uterque)
- ✗ Overdose

**Explanation:** OD = right eye (oculus dexter), OS = left eye (oculus sinister), OU = both eyes (oculus uterque). Similarly for ears: AD = right ear, AS = left ear, AU = both ears. Note: Some practices are moving toward writing "right eye" to avoid confusion.

---

### Q12.11
**The term "dyspnea" means:**
- ✓ Difficult or labored breathing
- ✗ Absence of breathing (that would be apnea)
- ✗ Rapid breathing (that would be tachypnea)
- ✗ Painful urination (that would be dysuria)

**Explanation:** Dys- = difficult/painful, -pnea = breathing. Dyspnea = difficult breathing. A- = without (apnea). Tachy- = fast (tachypnea). Brady- = slow (bradypnea).

---

### Q12.12
**The root word "nephr/o" refers to which organ?**
- ✓ Kidney
- ✗ Liver (that would be hepat/o)
- ✗ Lung (that would be pneum/o or pulmon/o)
- ✗ Heart (that would be cardi/o)

**Explanation:** Nephr/o = kidney (nephritis = kidney inflammation, nephrectomy = kidney removal). Also note: ren/o is another root for kidney (renal = pertaining to the kidney). Both roots are commonly used in veterinary medicine.

---

### Q12.13
**The term "lateral" means:**
- ✓ Away from the midline of the body
- ✗ Toward the midline (that would be medial)
- ✗ Toward the head (that would be cranial)
- ✗ Toward the ground/bottom surface (that would be ventral)

**Explanation:** Lateral = away from the midline (to the side). Medial = toward the midline. These terms are fundamental to describing anatomical locations, radiographic positioning, and surgical approaches.

---

### Q12.14
**The combining form "oste/o" refers to:**
- ✓ Bone
- ✗ Joint (that would be arthr/o)
- ✗ Muscle (that would be my/o)
- ✗ Cartilage (that would be chondr/o)

**Explanation:** Oste/o = bone. Examples: osteoarthritis (bone + joint inflammation), osteosarcoma (bone tumor), osteotomy (cutting bone). Arthr/o = joint, my/o = muscle, chondr/o = cartilage.

---

### Q12.15
**The suffix "-oma" means:**
- ✓ Tumor or mass
- ✗ Inflammation (that would be -itis)
- ✗ Pain (that would be -algia)
- ✗ Surgical repair (that would be -plasty)

**Explanation:** -oma = tumor/mass. Examples: lipoma (fatty tumor), melanoma (melanocyte tumor), osteosarcoma (malignant bone tumor), lymphoma (lymphoid tissue tumor). Not all -omas are malignant — a lipoma is benign.

**Explanation:** Acepromazine is a phenothiazine tranquilizer that lowers the seizure threshold, causes hypotension (caution in compromised patients), and has NO analgesic properties. It should be avoided in seizure patients, boxers (breed sensitivity), and patients in shock.

### Q2.13
**Purevax (Merial/Boehringer Ingelheim) feline rabies vaccine differs from traditional killed rabies vaccines in that it is:**

**Explanation:** kVp controls X-ray beam energy (penetrating power). Higher kVp produces more scatter radiation and more shades of gray (long-scale/low contrast). Lower kVp produces fewer grays and more black-and-white differences (short-scale/high contrast). mAs primarily controls density.
**Source:** Lavin's Radiography for Veterinary Technicians; Thrall's Textbook of Veterinary Diagnostic Radiology

---

### Q7.12
**Increasing mAs (milliampere-seconds) on a radiograph primarily affects:**
- ✓ Density (overall blackness/darkness of the image) — higher mAs = more photons = darker image
- ✗ Contrast primarily
- ✗ Magnification
- ✗ Image sharpness/detail

**Explanation:** mAs (mA × time) controls the quantity of X-rays produced. More photons reaching the detector produce a darker (more dense) image. mAs has minimal effect on contrast. The "golden rule": adjust mAs for density, adjust kVp for contrast.
**Source:** Lavin's Radiography for Veterinary Technicians; Thrall's Textbook of Veterinary Diagnostic Radiology

### Q7.13
**A grid is used in veterinary radiography to:**
- ✓ Absorb scatter radiation before it reaches the detector, improving image contrast (especially for body parts >10 cm thick)
- ✗ Protect the patient from radiation
- ✗ Increase the speed of the exposure
- ✗ Replace the need for collimation

**Explanation:** Grids contain thin lead strips that absorb scattered photons traveling at oblique angles. They are used for body parts thicker than 10 cm where scatter degrades contrast. Using a grid requires increased mAs (2-3x) to compensate for absorbed primary beam photons.
**Source:** Lavin's Radiography for Veterinary Technicians; Thrall's Textbook of Veterinary Diagnostic Radiology

### Q7.14
**For a VD (ventrodorsal) pelvis radiograph to evaluate hip dysplasia, the dog's hindlimbs should be:**
- ✓ Extended caudally, parallel to each other, with femurs internally rotated so patellas are centered over the trochlear grooves
- ✗ Flexed in a "frog-leg" position
- ✗ Pulled cranially alongside the chest
- ✗ Left in any natural position without specific positioning

### Q7.15
**The two most common transducer types used in veterinary ultrasonography are:**
- ✓ Linear and curvilinear (convex) array transducers
- ✗ Flat-panel and phased-array only
- ✗ Rotating mechanical and annular array
- ✗ CT and MRI transducers

**Explanation:** Linear transducers produce a rectangular image and are used for superficial structures (tendons, eyes, small patients). Curvilinear (convex) transducers produce a wider fan-shaped image and are used for abdominal imaging in medium-to-large dogs. Sector/phased-array probes are used for echocardiography.

### Q7.16
**Before an abdominal ultrasound, patient preparation typically includes:**

- ✗ Administering IV contrast agent
- ✗ Inflating the abdomen with CO2
- ✗ No preparation is needed — ultrasound works through hair

**Explanation:** Air trapped in hair prevents ultrasound transmission. Clipping and applying coupling gel ensures direct contact and optimal sound wave transmission. Alcohol can be used as a temporary coupling agent for quick scans but gel provides better images.

### Q7.17
**CT (computed tomography) imaging requires the veterinary patient to be:**
- ✓ Under general anesthesia to prevent motion artifact during the scan
- ✗ Conscious and standing
- ✗ Only mildly sedated — CT is tolerant of motion

### Q7.18
**The purpose of collimation in radiography is to:**
- ✓ Restrict the X-ray beam to only the area of clinical interest, reducing patient dose and scatter radiation while improving image contrast
- ✗ Increase the beam size to cover the entire patient

### Q8.16

### Q8.22
**DKA (diabetic ketoacidosis) in a dog or cat requires all of the following EXCEPT:**

- ✗ Enlargement of tissue (that would be -megaly)
- ✗ Softening of tissue (that would be -malacia)

**Explanation:** Dys- = abnormal/difficult, -plasia = formation/growth. Dysplasia = abnormal development. Examples: hip dysplasia (abnormal hip joint formation), elbow dysplasia, renal dysplasia. Distinguished from neoplasia (new growth/tumor) and hyperplasia (excessive normal growth).
**Source:** Medical Terminology for Veterinary Technicians (Romich); Merck Veterinary Manual

### Q12.20
**The term "neoplasia" means:**
- ✓ New, abnormal growth (tumor) — can be benign or malignant
- ✗ Cell death (that would be necrosis)
- ✗ Excessive normal cell growth (that would be hyperplasia)
- ✗ Decreased cell growth (that would be hypoplasia)

**Explanation:** Neo- = new, -plasia = growth/formation. Neoplasia encompasses all tumor types: benign (lipoma, adenoma) and malignant (carcinoma, sarcoma). Hyperplasia is increased but normal cell growth; neoplasia is autonomous, unregulated growth.
**Source:** Medical Terminology for Veterinary Technicians (Romich)

### Q12.21
**The abbreviation "PRN" means:**
- ✓ Pro re nata — "as needed" (given only when necessary based on symptoms)
- ✗ Per rectum nightly
- ✗ Pre-operative right now
- ✗ Post-renal nephritis

**Explanation:** PRN = pro re nata (as the situation demands). Used for medications given as-needed rather than on a fixed schedule. Example: "Tramadol 50 mg PO PRN for pain" means give only when the patient shows signs of pain, not on a scheduled basis.
**Source:** McCurnin's Clinical Textbook for Veterinary Technicians; Standard veterinary prescription abbreviations

### Q12.22
**The abbreviation "NPO" means:**
- ✓ Nil per os — nothing by mouth (the patient should not eat or drink)
- ✗ Normal post-operative
- ✗ No prescription ordered
- ✗ Non-pathogenic organism

**Explanation:** NPO = nil per os (nothing by mouth). Used pre-operatively (typically NPO for food 8-12 hours before anesthesia, water 2-4 hours) or for patients with GI conditions requiring bowel rest. Always confirm specific NPO instructions for each case.
**Source:** McCurnin's Clinical Textbook for Veterinary Technicians; Standard veterinary abbreviations

### Q12.23
**The directional term "palmar" refers to:**
- ✓ The caudal surface of the forelimb below the carpus (equivalent to the palm of the hand)
- ✗ The caudal surface of the hindlimb below the tarsus (that would be plantar)
- ✗ The dorsal surface of any limb
- ✗ Toward the nose

**Explanation:** Below the carpus (front limb): palmar = back surface, dorsal = front surface. Below the tarsus (hind limb): plantar = back surface, dorsal = front surface. These replace cranial/caudal terminology for the distal extremities.
**Source:** Medical Terminology for Veterinary Technicians (Romich); Veterinary Anatomy textbooks

### Q12.24
**The term "hepatomegaly" means:**
- ✓ Enlargement of the liver
- ✗ Inflammation of the liver (that would be hepatitis)
- ✗ Removal of the liver (that would be hepatectomy)
- ✗ A liver tumor (that would be hepatoma)

**Explanation:** Hepat/o = liver, -megaly = enlargement. Hepatomegaly = enlarged liver. Similarly: splenomegaly = enlarged spleen, cardiomegaly = enlarged heart. Causes include congestion, neoplasia, infection, and metabolic disease.
**Source:** Medical Terminology for Veterinary Technicians (Romich)

### Q12.25
**The prefix "poly-" means:**
- ✓ Many or excessive
- ✗ Few or deficient (that would be oligo-)
- ✗ Without (that would be a-)
- ✗ Painful (that would be dys-)

**Explanation:** Poly- = many/excessive. Examples: polydipsia (excessive drinking), polyuria (excessive urination), polyphagia (excessive eating), polycythemia (too many red blood cells). The opposite is oligo- (few/scanty): oliguria, oligodipsia.
**Source:** Medical Terminology for Veterinary Technicians (Romich)

## CATEGORY 13: SCOPE OF PRACTICE — WASHINGTON STATE (WAC 246-935-050)

### Q13.1
**Under Washington State law (WAC 246-935-050), which of the following is a licensed veterinary technician PROHIBITED from doing?**
- ✓ Diagnosing a disease or condition
- ✗ Inducing and maintaining anesthesia under direct supervision
- ✗ Placing an IV catheter
- ✗ Performing a fecal flotation

**Explanation:** WAC 246-935-050 explicitly prohibits veterinary technicians from: diagnosing, prognosing, prescribing drugs or appliances, and performing surgery (with limited exceptions). These are reserved for the licensed veterinarian.
**Source:** WAC 246-935-050 — https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=246-935-050

---

### Q13.2
**Under WAC 246-935-050, a licensed veterinary technician may induce and maintain anesthesia under which level of supervision?**
- ✓ Direct supervision — the veterinarian must be on the premises and quickly available
- ✗ No supervision — technicians can independently anesthetize patients
- ✗ Immediate supervision — the veterinarian must be physically present and watching
- ✗ Indirect supervision — the veterinarian need not be on premises

**Explanation:** Anesthesia induction and maintenance requires direct supervision (veterinarian on premises, quickly available but not necessarily watching). This distinguishes it from tasks like dental extractions which require immediate supervision (vet physically present and observing).
**Source:** WAC 246-935-050 — https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=246-935-050

---

### Q13.3
**Under WAC 246-935-050, dental extractions performed by a licensed veterinary technician require:**
- ✓ Immediate supervision — the veterinarian must be physically present and directly observing
- ✗ No supervision
- ✗ Indirect supervision only
- ✗ Dental extractions cannot be performed by technicians under any circumstances

**Explanation:** Dental extractions are one of the highest-supervision tasks allowed for LVTs in Washington State, requiring immediate supervision (veterinarian physically present and observing the procedure). This is the most restrictive supervision level.
**Source:** WAC 246-935-050 — https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=246-935-050

---

### Q13.4
**A veterinarian leaves the building for lunch. Under WAC 246-935-050, which task can a licensed veterinary technician still perform?**
- ✓ IV injection into an uncatheterized vein (requires only indirect supervision — vet available for consultation)
- ✗ Inducing anesthesia on the next surgical patient (requires direct supervision)
- ✗ Performing a dental extraction (requires immediate supervision)
- ✗ Diagnosing the next patient's condition (prohibited for technicians)

**Explanation:** Indirect supervision means the veterinarian is not necessarily on premises but is available for consultation (e.g., by phone). Tasks like IV injections into uncatheterized veins, diagnostic procedures (fecals, urinalysis, ECG), and bladder catheterization only require indirect supervision.
**Source:** WAC 246-935-050 — https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=246-935-050

---

### Q13.5
**Under WAC 246-935-050, an unregistered veterinary assistant (non-credentialed staff) may:**
- ✓ Perform tasks delegated by the veterinarian under at least indirect supervision by a veterinarian or licensed veterinary technician, EXCEPT tasks specifically restricted to LVTs
- ✗ Perform all tasks identical to a licensed veterinary technician
- ✗ Work completely independently without any supervision
- ✗ Diagnose and prescribe if trained on the job

**Explanation:** Unregistered assistants work under at least indirect supervision and may perform general tasks (restraint, cleaning, feeding, taking vitals). However, they CANNOT perform tasks specifically restricted to licensed veterinary technicians (anesthesia, dental extractions, certain injections, etc.).
**Source:** WAC 246-935-050 — https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=246-935-050

---

### Q13.6
**Under WAC 246-935-050, who may prescribe medications in Washington State?**
- ✓ Only the licensed veterinarian — technicians and assistants are explicitly prohibited from prescribing
- ✗ Licensed veterinary technicians with 5+ years experience
- ✗ Any staff member trained by the veterinarian
- ✗ Veterinary assistants under indirect supervision

**Explanation:** Prescribing drugs or appliances is one of the explicitly prohibited acts for all non-veterinarian staff, regardless of their level of training, licensure, or experience. Only a licensed veterinarian (DVM/VMD) may prescribe.
**Source:** WAC 246-935-050 — https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=246-935-050

---

### Q13.7
**Under WAC 246-935-050, a licensed veterinary technician may suture skin or gingiva under which supervision level?**
- ✓ Direct supervision — the veterinarian must be on the premises and quickly available
- ✗ No supervision
- ✗ Immediate supervision
- ✗ Technicians cannot suture under any circumstances

**Explanation:** Suturing skin or gingiva is permitted for licensed veterinary technicians under direct supervision (veterinarian on premises, quickly available). This is a technical procedure that requires the vet to be accessible for questions or complications but doesn't require direct observation.
**Source:** WAC 246-935-050 — https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=246-935-050

---

### Q13.8
**Under Washington State law, a veterinary technician may initiate treatment on a patient without prior veterinary instruction ONLY in:**
- ✓ An emergency situation where the animal's life is in immediate danger and the veterinarian is not available
- ✗ Any situation where the tech feels confident
- ✗ Routine appointments that follow standard protocols
- ✗ Technicians can never initiate any treatment

**Explanation:** WAC 246-935-050 prohibits technicians from initiating treatment without prior instruction EXCEPT in life-threatening emergencies when the veterinarian cannot be reached. This exception recognizes that delaying treatment could result in patient death.
**Source:** WAC 246-935-050 — https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=246-935-050

### Q13.9
**Under WAC 246-935-050, which of the following requires only INDIRECT supervision for a licensed veterinary technician?**
- ✓ Performing a cystocentesis (bladder centesis)
- ✗ Inducing general anesthesia
- ✗ Performing a dental extraction
- ✗ Diagnosing a patient's condition

**Explanation:** Centesis procedures (abdominal, thoracic, cystocentesis), including fine needle aspirates, require only indirect supervision for licensed veterinary technicians in Washington State. The veterinarian need not be on premises but must be available for consultation.
**Source:** WAC 246-935-050 — https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=246-935-050

### Q13.10
**Under WAC 246-935-050, blood product administration requires which level of supervision?**
- ✓ Direct supervision — the veterinarian must be on the premises and quickly available
- ✗ Indirect supervision
- ✗ No supervision required
- ✗ Only veterinarians may administer blood products

**Explanation:** Administering blood products (transfusions) carries significant risk for adverse reactions and requires direct supervision. The veterinarian must be on premises and quickly available to manage potential transfusion reactions.
**Source:** WAC 246-935-050 — https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=246-935-050

### Q13.14
